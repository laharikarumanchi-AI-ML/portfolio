## Problem framing

You're designing the **home feed** for LinkedIn: when a user opens the app, they see a chronological-ish stream of posts from people in their network. The hard part is *not* showing the posts — it's deciding **where the per-user feed gets assembled** and **when**.

Two classic poles:

- **Fan-out-on-write (push):** When Alice posts, immediately push her post into the feed-inbox of every connection. Reads are cheap (one lookup), writes are expensive (multiplied by connection count).
- **Fan-out-on-read (pull):** When Bob opens his feed, fetch posts from each of his connections and merge them. Writes are cheap (one row), reads are expensive (many fetches + a merge).

The real LinkedIn (and Twitter, and Facebook) ship **hybrids**. The interesting question is: where does the threshold go, and why?

## Requirements

**Functional**

- Users can post text + image; posts are visible to their direct connections (1st-degree) and optionally their network (2nd-degree).
- Home feed shows the most recent ~50 posts from the user's network, ordered chronologically (ranking out of scope here).
- Users can connect / unconnect; the feed reflects the current connection set with eventual consistency (a few seconds is fine).

**Non-functional**

| Constraint | Target |
|---|---|
| Users | ~1B registered, ~300M MAU |
| Median connections / user | ~400 (long tail to ~30k for influencers) |
| Home feed read latency | p99 < 200 ms |
| Post-write to feed visibility | < 30 seconds for non-celebrity authors |
| Read:write ratio on feed | ~100:1 (much more reading than posting) |
| Consistency | Eventual is acceptable for the feed itself; strong for the post record |

## Capacity estimation

Back-of-envelope:

| Quantity | Estimate | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Posts per day | 5 M | ~1.7% of MAU post on a given day, some post 2× |
| Fan-out writes per day (push, naive) | 2 B | 5M × 400 avg connections |
| Storage growth (feed entries, push model) | ~80 GB/day | 2B × 40-byte index entries |
| Feed reads per day | 600 M | 300M MAU × ~2 feed loads each |
| Peak feed-read QPS | ~15 k | with 5× peak-to-average ratio |

The 80 GB/day write amplification under a naive push model is the **first reason** you don't push to everyone.

Layers from top to bottom:

- **Edge** — clients hit a CDN for static assets and an API gateway (auth, rate-limiting, routing) for everything else.
- **Services** — stateless, horizontally scalable. The five shown are the minimum to demo the feed path; messaging / notifications / job search are real services elided here.
- **Async** — a Kafka topic (`post-created`) decouples post writes from fan-out. A dedicated worker pool drains it.
- **Storage** — separate datastores by access pattern: `PostDB` (sharded SQL or Cassandra, post records), `Connection Graph` (graph DB or sharded SQL, edges + 2nd-degree precomputes), `FeedDB` (per-user materialized inbox — *only for non-celebrities*; see deep dive), `UserDB`, `Object Store` (S3 / GCS for images).
- **Caching** — `Feed Cache` (Redis, hot users' assembled feeds, 60-second TTL); `Profile Cache` (Redis, profile snippets used in feed rendering).

## Data model (sketch)

```sql
-- Posts
posts(post_id PK, author_id, body, image_url?, created_at, visibility)
-- Sharded by author_id; secondary index on author_id + created_at.

-- Connections (symmetric)
connections(user_a, user_b, status, created_at, PRIMARY KEY (user_a, user_b))
-- Stored bidirectionally for O(1) "who follows me" reads.

-- FeedDB: materialized inbox per user (push targets only)
feed_entries(user_id, post_id, author_id, created_at,
             PRIMARY KEY (user_id, created_at DESC, post_id))
-- Sharded by user_id; rolling window — drop entries older than 7 days.
```

The `FeedDB` schema is the crux. We only populate it for users whose authors are *under* the celebrity threshold — the deep dive explains why.

## Deep dive: feed generation

This is the part that determines whether your design scales or quietly explodes.

### The two extremes

| | **Fan-out on write** | **Fan-out on read** |
|---|---|---|
| When the work happens | At post time | At feed-load time |
| Write cost (per post) | O(followers) — could be millions | O(1) |
| Read cost (per feed load) | O(1) lookup in pre-built inbox | O(connections) fetches + merge |
| Storage cost | High (each post duplicated per follower) | Low |
| Latency of feed read | ~10 ms | ~200–500 ms |
| Bad at | Celebrities (write amplification) | Active feed readers (read amplification) |
| Good at | Read-heavy workloads with bounded follower counts | Write-heavy workloads with sporadic readers |

LinkedIn's read:write is ~100:1, which biases the choice strongly toward push. But the **influencer problem** breaks the simple push model: an account with 1 M followers triggers 1 M writes per post, often in a thundering herd. You also waste those writes on inactive accounts who never read.

### The hybrid

The pattern that ships at every social-feed-scale company is roughly the same:

1. **Push (fan-out on write) for the long tail of normal users.** When Alice (400 connections) posts, the fan-out worker writes 400 entries to `FeedDB`. Reads for her connections are O(1).
2. **Pull (fan-out on read) for celebrities.** Define a `celebrity_threshold` (e.g., > 50k followers). For accounts above it, *do not* push. Instead, when Bob opens his feed, the Feed Service queries `FeedDB` for the pushed posts AND issues a separate query for "recent posts by any celebrity I follow" (a small number), then merges in memory.
3. **Cap the inbox.** A user's `FeedDB` rows are pruned to the last 7 days or last 1000 entries, whichever is smaller. Old posts re-enter via pull only.
4. **Lazy fan-out for cold users.** If a user hasn't opened the app in 30 days, skip the push for them entirely. Re-warm their inbox on next login.

This eliminates the write amplification at the celebrity tail while keeping the median feed read cheap.

### Failure modes worth naming

- **Fan-out worker backlog.** A celebrity who's *just under* the threshold posts → 49k writes hit the worker in seconds. Queue depth spikes, fan-out lag grows. Mitigation: scale workers on Kafka lag; partition by author shard so one slow author doesn't block others.
- **Connection graph staleness.** Bob disconnects from Alice; her post still arrives in his inbox for ~5 seconds. Acceptable for a feed; the post simply doesn't appear on his next refresh.
- **Hot feed cache invalidation.** A trending post in a cached feed causes thundering herd of cache misses. Mitigation: stale-while-revalidate; serve last-known cache for 1s while a single re-fetch happens.

## What this design deliberately doesn't cover

- **Ranking.** Real LinkedIn doesn't show strictly chronological feeds — there's a personalized ranker (recency, engagement prediction, network strength) layered on top. That's a deep dive in its own right.
- **Search.** Inverted indexes for people / jobs / companies, autocomplete, relevance — out of scope.
- **Messaging.** Real-time presence + WebSocket fan-out — different architecture entirely.
- **Notifications.** The notification queue is shown in the diagram but unwired here.
- **Geographic distribution.** Multi-region, conflict resolution, regional caches — assumed away with "we're in one region."

Each of these is interview-deep on its own; trying to cover them all is the textbook trap.
