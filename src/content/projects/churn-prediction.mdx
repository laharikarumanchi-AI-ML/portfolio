## Problem

Churn is one of the canonical applied-ML problems: imbalanced classes, mixed feature types, and a downstream user (the retention team) who cares as much about *why* a customer is predicted to churn as about the prediction itself. The goal was a model that hit a respectable ROC-AUC and produced explanations the business could act on.

## Approach

Project at a glance:

- Telco customer churn dataset, with standard preprocessing: one-hot for categoricals, scaling for numerics, sensible imputation.
- **XGBoost** classifier as the primary model.
- Class imbalance handled with `scale_pos_weight` set from the empirical ratio rather than naive oversampling.
- Evaluation on a held-out test set with ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, and confusion matrices — not just accuracy, which is misleading at this base rate.
- **SHAP** values for global and per-customer feature attribution.

## Results

- **0.83 ROC-AUC** on the held-out test set.
- SHAP analysis identified **contract type** (month-to-month customers churn the most) and **tenure** (short-tenure customers churn the most) as the dominant drivers — both intuitive and actionable for retention campaigns.
- PR-AUC and confusion matrices gave a clearer picture than accuracy alone of where the model was confident and where it was hedging.

## What I'd do differently

I'd run a calibration check (reliability diagram + Brier score) — for retention decisions the *probability* matters as much as the rank, and untreated XGBoost outputs aren't well-calibrated by default. Isotonic or Platt scaling on a held-out slice would be a one-evening addition with real business value.
